Every Child Deserves a Family Act

Preamble Article I Article II
Status Cosponsors Senate House Signatures

Preamble

This bill will prohibit discrimination in adoption or foster care placements based on the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of any prospective adoptive or foster parent, or that of the child involved. It will become effective immediately after Congressional passage and Presidential signature.

Article I: Rationale

Section 1: Findings

After researching, the following has been concluded:

  1. There is a shortage of qualified individuals willing to adopt or foster a child in the child welfare system. As a result, thousands of children lack a permanent and/or safe home.
  2. In order to open more homes for foster children, child welfare agencies should eliminate sexual orientation, gender identity, and marital status discrimination and bias in adoption and foster care recruitment, selection, and placement.
  3. Of the estimated 427,910 children in the United States foster care system, at least 100,000 cannot return to their families and are legally free for adoption.
    1. In 2015, 53,549 children were adopted, while 20,789 ‘aged out’ of the system.
    2. Research shows that youth who ‘age out’ of the system are at a high risk of poverty, homelessness, incarceration, and early parenthood.
    3. Increasing adoption rates, in addition to establishing permanency and decreasing the risks of for foster youth, can yield annual national savings between $3.3-6.3 billion.
  4. Experts agree that lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, etc. youth experience discrimination, harassment, and violence in the foster care system because of their status.
  5. Approximately 60% of homeless LGBT youth were previously in foster care. According to the Urban Justice Center, many of these young people say they feel ‘safer’ living on the streets than in foster care.
  6. According to the Williams Institute, around 19% of same-sex couple households include children under 18 years of age.
  7. As of 2013, same-sex couples are raising 1.4% of adopted children with 2 parents, and are fostering 1.7% of foster children living with 2 parents. A 2007 Williams Institute report found that an additional 2,000,000 gay, lesbian, and bisexual individuals are interested in adoption.
  8. The Williams Institute/Urban Institute found that same-sex couples raising adopted children tend to be: older than, just as educated as, and have access to the same economic resources as other adoptive parents. Studies confirm that children with same-sex parents have the same advantages and same expectations for health, social, and psychological adjustment, and development as children whose parents are heterosexual.
    1. Barring applicants for adoption and foster parenthood on the basis of sexual orientation, gender identity, and marital status has resulted in reducing the number of qualified adoptive and foster parents. This also, in many cases, prevents qualified LGBT applicants from adopting their own relatives out of the system after a parental death.
    2. As of 2013, the Williams Institute found, more than 3,400 children currently live in a foster home with a same-sex couple. Another 22,000 children are being raised by same-sex adoptive couples. If these discriminatory practices spread, they estimate spending to increase by between $87-130 million yearly to compensate.
  9. Some states allow a single member of an unmarried couple to adopt, but refuse to acknowledge the second parent as the legal parent of the child. Recognition of joint adoption by unmarried couples provides children with the same rights and security that other children enjoy, and provide access to the second parent’s health benefits, survivor’s, Social Security, and child support entitlements to the child. They also enable both parents to consent to medical care, education, and other important decisions of permanency for parents and children.
  10. After research showed that sexual orientation is a non-determinative factor in parental success, professional organizations in the fields of medicine, psychology, law, and child welfare have taken official positions in support of the ability of qualified gay, lesbian, bisexual, and unmarried couples to foster and/or adopt children.
  11. Several states have “conscience clause” laws that permit agencies and case workers to delay or deny placements to anyone with whom they have a religious or moral objection.
  12. Discrimination against qualified potential foster or adoptive parents is not in the best interests of foster children.

Article II: Every Child Deserves a Family

Section 1: Activities

  1. PROHIBITION - An entity that receives Federal assistance or contracts with an entity that receives Federal assistance, and is involved in adoption or foster care placements may not:
    1. Deny to any person the opportunity to become an adoptive or foster parent on the basis of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of the person, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child involved;
    2. Delay or deny the placement of a child for adoption or into foster care on the basis of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of any prospective adoptive or foster parent, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child; or
    3. Require different or additional screenings, processes, or procedures for adoptive or foster placement decisions on the basis of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of the prospective adoptive or foster parent, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child involved.
  2. DEFINITION OF PLACEMENT DECISION - In this section, the term “placement decision” means the decision to place, or to delay or deny the placement of, a child in a foster care or an adoptive home, and includes the decision of the agency or entity involved to seek the termination of birth parent rights or otherwise make a child legally available for adoptive placement.

Section 2: Equitable Relief

Any individual who is aggrieved by an action in violation of Article II, Section I may bring an action seeking relief in a United States district court of appropriate jurisdiction.

Section 3: Technical Assistance

In order to ensure compliance with, and ensure understanding of the legal, practice, and culture changes required by, this Act in making foster care and adoption placement decisions, the Attorney General shall provide technical assistance to all entities covered by this Act, including:

  1. Identifying laws and regulations inconsistent with this Act and providing guidance and training to ensure the practices and procedures are brought into compliance within the prescribed period of time;
  2. Identifying casework practices and procedures inconsistent with this Act and providing guidance and training to ensure the practices and procedures are brought into compliance within the prescribed period of time;
  3. Providing guidance in expansion of recruitment efforts to ensure consideration of all interested and qualified prospective adoptive and foster parents regardless of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of the prospective parent;
  4. Comprehensive cultural competency training for covered entities and prospective adoptive and foster parents; and
  5. Training judges and attorneys involved in foster care and adoption cases on the findings and purposes of this Act.

Section 4: Deadline For Compliance

  1. IN GENERAL - An entity that receives Federal Assistance and is involved with adoption or foster care placements shall comply with this Act not later than 6 months after passage of this Act.
  2. AUTHORITY TO EXTEND DEADLINE - If a State demonstrates to the Attorney General that it is necessary to extend the deadline beyond the allotted 6 month timeframe on the basis of further pre-emptive technical assistance, the Attorney General can extend the deadline up to 60 days after the effective compliance date of this Act.
  3. AUTHORITY TO WITHHOLD FUNDS - If a State fails to comply with this Act, the Federal Government is entitled to withhold payment to the State of amounts otherwise payable to the State under part B or E of title IV of the Social Security Act, to the extent deemed necessary to induce the State into compliance with the Act.

Section 5: GAO Review

  1. IN GENERAL - Not later than 5 years after the date of enactment of this Act, the Government Accountability (GAO) shall conduct a study to determine whether the States have substantially complied with this Act, including specifically whether the States have:
    1. Eliminated policies, practices, or statutes that deny to any otherwise qualifies person the opportunity to become an adoptive or foster parent on the basis of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of the person, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child involved;
    2. removed all program, policy, or statutory barriers that delay or deny the placement of a child for adoption or into foster care on the basis of the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of any qualified, prospective adoptive or foster parent, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child; and
    3. eliminated all different or additional screenings, processes, or procedures for adoptive or foster placement decisions based on the sexual orientation, gender identity, or marital status of the prospective adoptive or foster parent, or the sexual orientation or gender identity of the child involved.
  2. REPORT TO CONGRESS - Not later than 1 year after completing the study required by paragraph 1 of this section, the GAO shall submit to Congress a written report that contains the results of the study.
