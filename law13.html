The Net Neutrality Protection Act

Preamble Article I
Status Cosponsors House Senate Signatures

Preamble

In order to safeguard the free and open Internet, this bill shall prevent the Federal Communications Committee (FCC) from infringing on the people’s right to net neutrality, the principle that Internet service providers should enable access to all content and applications regardless of the source, and without favoring or blocking particular products or websites.

Article I: Nullification of the FCC’s Attempt to Destroy the Free and Open Internet

Section 1: Definition and Identification of Net Neutrality

Net neutrality is the principle that Internet service providers should enable access to all content and applications regardless of the source, and without favoring or blocking particular products or websites. Here are a few examples of what can happen without net neutrality:

  1. ISPs can start treating certain sources or kinds of content differently than others, therefore they can slow down websites that they do not like, or websites that are not paying them.
  2. ISPs would be able to charge you for certain websites. For example, on TV, you can buy certain bundles of channels. ISPs would be able to charge you $99 a month for the social media bundle, $499 a month for the video streaming bundle, $299 a month for the online shopping bundle, etc.
  3. ISPs would be able to prevent any access to websites they do not approve of. For example, if an article negatively talks about an ISP, the ISP can slow down/eliminate all access to this website.
  4. ISPs could make websites pay for user access to the website. For example, if an ISP made all websites pay money, wealthy companies like Google and Facebook could pay exorbitant fees, but anyone trying to start a business with a small website might not be able to pay.

Section 2: Nullification of the FCC GN Docket No. 14–28

On the 18th of May 2017, a rule was proposed to destroy net neutrality. This rule shall have no effect and the FCC may not reissue this rule or any other rule to destroy net neutrality in the future.
